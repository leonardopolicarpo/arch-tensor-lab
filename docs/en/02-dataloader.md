# 02: The DataLoader and Batch Dynamics

> 🌐 **Available in other languages:** [Português (Brasil)](../pt-br/02-dataloader.md)

Here I detail the implementation of the **DataLoader**, the component responsible for slicing the binary dataset and organizing the training flow into multidimensional matrices (Tensors).

---

## 1. The Slicing Concept

For the model to learn, I cannot feed it the entire 3.5MB file at once. I had to create a system that extracts small random sequences for parallel processing.

### Defined Hyperparameters:
- **`batch_size` (32):** How many sequences we process simultaneously. This is what allows us to leverage the processor's vector power.
- **`block_size` (64):** The "context window" size. The network looks at 64 characters to try and predict the 65th.

---

## 2. Sampling and Stacking Logic

The process of transforming a linear data ribbon into a training matrix involves three fundamental steps:

### A. Index Sampling (`torch.randint`)
I used the `randint` function to generate random starting points within the tensor. To prevent slicing from attempting to read data beyond the end of the file (causing index errors), I applied a **safety margin**:
`max_limit = len(data) - block_size - 1`.

### B. X and Y Offsets
The neural network learns through next-character prediction. Therefore, for every input **X**, I generated a target **Y** which is the exact same sequence, but shifted one index to the right.

### C. The "Stapler" (`torch.stack`)
Each sliced sequence is initially a 1D tensor. `torch.stack` acts like a stapler, stacking these 32 sequences vertically to form a **32x64** matrix. This optimizes mathematical computation, allowing the CPU to process the entire block at once.

---

## 3. Mathematical Visualization

For a simplified example of `batch_size=2` and `block_size=4`, the structure generated by my DataLoader follows this model:

**Input (X):**
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 
10 & 22 & 15 & 40 \\
55 & 12 & 33 & 08 
\end{bmatrix}
$$

**Target (Y):**
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 
22 & 15 & 40 & 11 \\
12 & 33 & 08 & 99 
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Thus, when the network finds token **10**, it is trained to know that the next one should be **22**. When it sees the sequence **10, 22, 15**, the target is **40**.

---

## 4. Memory Efficiency

By loading the `.pt` file with `weights_only=True`, I ensured that only raw data was allocated to RAM/CPU. Using `int64` (Long) tensors is vital here, as PyTorch's loss functions and embedding layers require this format for indexing.

---

## 5. Insights

During the construction of the DataLoader, it became clear that the tokens (`int64`) I am manipulating are, in fact, memory addresses for what comes next:

1.  **The Embedding Table:** Each ID will serve to "fish" for a vector of decimal numbers (e.g., $v = [0.12, -0.5, \dots]$) representing the character in a vector space.
2.  **Connection to VectorDBs:** This logic is the foundation of vector databases. While my DataLoader prepares indices for *prediction*, a VectorDB uses these same types of vectors for *semantic search* (finding texts with similar meanings).
3.  **Scalability:** Understanding this at the character level gives me the foundation to implement tokenization by words or sub-words (BPE) in the future, where the extracted "meaning" will be much denser.
